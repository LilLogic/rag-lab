A Retrieval-Augmented Generation system combines information retrieval techniques with large language models to generate responses grounded in external knowledge sources. Instead of relying exclusively on the internal parameters of a pretrained model, the system dynamically retrieves relevant information at inference time and injects it into the prompt context. This architecture enables the model to answer questions using information that may be recent, proprietary, or domain-specific.

Traditional language models generate text based on statistical patterns learned during training. While this provides strong generalization capabilities, it also introduces limitations related to outdated knowledge and factual hallucinations. RAG addresses these limitations by coupling generative reasoning with an external retrieval layer capable of accessing structured or unstructured datasets in real time.

The RAG workflow typically involves several sequential stages including ingestion, preprocessing, chunking, embedding generation, vector indexing, retrieval, reranking, and response generation. Each component contributes to the overall quality of the final answer. Weaknesses in any individual stage can negatively impact downstream performance and answer reliability.

Modern enterprise AI systems frequently adopt RAG architectures because they provide a practical mechanism for connecting language models to internal documentation, operational knowledge bases, or continuously evolving information systems. This makes RAG one of the foundational architectural patterns in production-grade AI applications.
