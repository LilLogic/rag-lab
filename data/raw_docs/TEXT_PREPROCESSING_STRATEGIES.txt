Text preprocessing improves the consistency and quality of data before embeddings are generated. Raw text often contains noisy artifacts such as duplicated headers, navigation menus, malformed characters, inconsistent spacing, or irrelevant boilerplate sections. Preprocessing aims to remove these sources of noise while preserving semantic meaning.

Sentence segmentation and paragraph normalization are common preprocessing steps. These operations improve chunk coherence and reduce fragmentation during downstream indexing. In multilingual systems, preprocessing may additionally include language detection, translation normalization, or tokenization strategies adapted to specific linguistic structures.

Normalization operations such as lowercasing, unicode standardization, punctuation cleanup, and whitespace reduction are frequently applied to improve embedding consistency. Without normalization, semantically identical content may produce unnecessarily divergent embeddings due to formatting inconsistencies.

The preprocessing stage directly affects retrieval performance because embedding models are highly sensitive to textual quality. Poor preprocessing can lead to degraded semantic representations, weaker retrieval precision, and lower relevance ranking during similarity search operations.
